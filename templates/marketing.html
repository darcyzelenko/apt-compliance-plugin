The problem

Compliance checking is not why you became an architect.

Every apartment project in Victoria and NSW requires careful checking against the Apartment Design Guidelines — bedroom dimensions, living area widths, storage volumes, daylight factors, open space, door widths, room depth, ventilation paths. Clause by clause, room by room.

In practice, this means hours of cross-referencing drawings against planning requirements — work that is essential, but repetitive. Work that happens at the expense of the design thinking, spatial reasoning and creative problem-solving that defines good architecture.

It happens early in design when decisions are still fluid, and again at DA lodgement when errors are expensive. Every hour spent checking is an hour not spent designing.

01
It is slow and error-prone by hand
Checking 12+ requirements across multiple rooms, manually measuring from drawings and cross-referencing schedules, creates opportunity for mistakes — particularly late in a project when time pressure is highest.
02
It happens too late in the process
Most compliance checking happens at DA stage, after design decisions have been locked in. Problems discovered late are expensive. A continuous feedback loop during schematic design changes the conversation entirely.
03
The guidelines are genuinely complex
ADG VIC and NSW ADG have different requirements. BCA accessibility requirements overlay both. Best practice thresholds exist beyond minimum compliance. Keeping all of this in your head while designing is a significant cognitive load.
04
Junior staff time is poorly spent on it
Compliance checking is typically delegated to junior architects and graduates — work that is important enough to get right, but not the best use of developing professional judgment.
